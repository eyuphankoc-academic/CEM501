## Owner's Representative — Deniz Akar

## Architect — Selin Yilmaz

### What only the Architect knew:

- The RFI backlog was largely her firm's fault. Her **senior project architect quit 5 weeks ago** and the replacement was still ramping up. She was instructed **not to admit this publicly** — instead, she would frame the delays as multi-party coordination issues.
- The **curtain wall delay was partly caused by her firm**. She issued a **design revision in Month 5** that changed mullion spacing on the south and east elevations (for energy code compliance). This forced the German manufacturer to re-engineer and re-fabricate custom profiles, adding an estimated **2-3 weeks** to manufacturing. The GC had been blaming the manufacturer exclusively.
- She **had not reviewed CO #07 at all**. Her firm was behind on billing and stretched thin. Her plan was to request backup documentation and commit to a recommendation in 10 days — buying time without admitting inaction.
- She was concerned the lab ventilation redesign would require a **structural modification to the roof framing** that had not been evaluated. She also saw it as a **fee opportunity** — additional design services beyond the original scope.

### What this means for the debrief:

- Did anyone uncover the architect's role in the curtain wall delay?
- Did the RFI discussion reveal the staffing problem, or did the architect successfully deflect?
- Did the architect admit she hadn't reviewed CO #07, or did she bluff through it?
- Was the architect under pressure from multiple directions simultaneously? How did she handle it?

## Cross-Cutting Dynamics — Discussion Guide

### 1. Information Withholding

The GC PM was withholding the curtain wall shipment, the MEP delay, and padding on CO #07. The Architect was hiding the staffing problem and her role in the curtain wall delay. The MEP Sub was hiding the crew reallocation. The Superintendent knew about the shipment and the MEP delay but was loyal to her PM.

**Question for the class:** When is withholding information a legitimate negotiation tactic, and when does it cross the line into bad faith? What are the contractual consequences if the curtain wall cover-up were discovered weeks later?

### 2. The Architect Under Siege

The Architect was the bottleneck on RFIs, the cause of the missing sleeve locations, a contributing factor in the curtain wall delay, and unprepared on CO #07 — all while hiding a staffing crisis.

**Question for the class:** Would it have been better for the Architect to admit the staffing problem? What would that have cost her in credibility? What did hiding it cost her in the meeting?

### 3. GC Internal Tension

The GC PM and Superintendent are on the same team but have fundamentally different approaches to transparency. The PM is strategic and selective with information. The Superintendent is a straight shooter who is uncomfortable with concealment.

**Question for the class:** In a real project, how do you manage this tension within your own organization? What happens when your field team and your office team have different instincts about what to share?

### 4. Everyone's Angle on the Lab Ventilation

Every party except the Superintendent had a financial incentive to encourage the fume hood addition. The Owner couldn't refuse it (political). The Architect saw a fee opportunity. The GC saw a major change order. The MEP Sub saw 400-600K in work. Only the Superintendent raised the practical constraint — the 6-week roofing deadline.

**Question for the class:** When everyone in the room has a reason to say yes, who represents the project's best interest? Is that the Owner's job? The chair's job? Nobody's job?

### 5. Quality of Action Items

**Question for the class:** Look at the minutes from the meeting. How many action items have a **name**, a **what**, and a **by when**? How many are vague ("someone will look into it")? Would these minutes hold up as a contractual record under the silence-is-acceptance clause?
