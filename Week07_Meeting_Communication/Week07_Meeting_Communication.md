## Part I: Meetings in CEM — The Most Expensive Communication Channel

---

### 1.1 How Much Time Goes to Meetings

| Finding | Source |
|---------|--------|
| Executives spend nearly **23 hours/week** in meetings — up from <10 hours in the 1960s | HBR, "Stop the Meeting Madness" (2017) |
| **71%** of 182 senior managers said meetings are unproductive and inefficient | HBR, "Stop the Meeting Madness" (2017) |
| Weekly meeting time on Microsoft Teams **increased 153%** since February 2020 | Microsoft Work Trend Index (2022) |

*Sources: [HBR: Stop the Meeting Madness (2017)](https://hbr.org/2017/07/stop-the-meeting-madness); [Microsoft Work Trend Index](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/worklab/work-trend-index)*

---

### 1.2 The Unproductive Majority

**Atlassian's 2024 "Workplace Woes: Meetings" report** — surveying 5,000 knowledge workers across four continents (with Wakefield Research):

- Meetings are **ineffective at their stated purpose 72% of the time** — whether for sharing information, encouraging collaboration, or accomplishing tasks
- **80%** of respondents said they would be more productive with fewer meetings

*Sources: [Atlassian: Workplace Woes — Meetings (2024)](https://www.atlassian.com/blog/workplace-woes-meetings); [Fortune: Meetings Are a Productivity Killer (March 21, 2024)](https://fortune.com/2024/03/21/meetings-productivity-ineffective-atlassian-report/)*

---

### 1.3 The Dollar Cost

**A Bain & Company case study** of a Fortune 500 company found:

- A **single weekly executive committee meeting** generated approximately **300,000 hours of support and preparation time per year** across the organization

*Source: [HBR: This Weekly Meeting Took Up 300,000 Hours a Year (2014)](https://hbr.org/2014/04/how-a-weekly-meeting-took-up-300000-hours-a-year)*

---

### Now Apply This to Construction

An OAC meeting with 6 people at fully loaded rates of $80–$150/hour:

| | Low Estimate | High Estimate |
|---|---|---|
| **Cost per 90-min meeting** | $720 | $1,350 |
| **Cost over a 2-year project** (~100 meetings) | $72,000 | $135,000 |

And that is **before** counting travel time, preparation, and the downstream cost of missed decisions.

> **Takeaway:** A 90-minute meeting that accomplishes nothing just burned $720–$1,350 in direct labor. Multiply by 100 meetings and poorly run OACs alone cost six figures.

---

### 1.4 The Meeting Paradox

**Meetings are essential:**
- Construction is multi-party, multi-disciplinary
- The OAC meeting is often the **only forum** where owner, designer, and builder are in the same room
- You cannot coordinate a $50M project through email alone

**But most meetings are poorly run:**
- ~72% fail to accomplish their purpose (Atlassian, 2024)
- Action items from meetings have notoriously poor follow-through rates

> **Takeaway:** The goal is not fewer meetings. The goal is to **make every meeting worth the six-figure annual investment** your project is making in them. That requires skill — before, during, and after.

## Part II: Types of CEM Meetings

---

### Quick Reference

| Meeting Type | Frequency | Key Feature |
|-------------|-----------|------------|
| **OAC** (Owner–Architect–Contractor) | Weekly/biweekly | Formal decisions with contractual force |
| **Pre-Bid** | Once during bidding | Prospective bidders tour site, ask questions |
| **Pre-Construction** | Once at kickoff | Establishes protocols and roles |
| **Safety / Toolbox Talk** | Daily or weekly | 10–15 min briefings on current hazards |
| **Progress / Look-Ahead** | Weekly (internal) | 3-week schedule coordination |

---

### 2.1 OAC Meeting (Owner–Architect–Contractor)

**Purpose:** The formal coordination meeting between the three primary contracting parties. Project-level decisions are made and officially recorded here.

**Typical Attendees:**
- Owner's representative (decision-making authority)
- Project architect (and/or engineer of record)
- GC project manager (usually chairs)
- GC superintendent (field perspective)
- Key subcontractors (as needed)
- Construction manager / owner's PM consultant (if applicable)

*Source: [Procore: OAC Meetings — Improving Collaboration & Project Efficiency](https://www.procore.com/library/oac-meeting)*

---

### Standard OAC Agenda

1. Review and approval of previous meeting minutes
2. Schedule status — milestone progress, critical path updates
3. Open RFIs — status, responses needed, aging items
4. Submittals — pending, approved, rejected, resubmit required
5. Change orders — proposed, pending, approved
6. Budget / cost status — contingency drawdown, payment applications
7. Safety — incidents, near-misses, upcoming high-risk activities
8. Quality — inspection results, nonconformances
9. New business / open discussion
10. Action items recap and next meeting date

**Tone:** Formal. Everything said is documented. **Decisions have contractual force.**

> **Takeaway:** The OAC meeting is the single most consequential recurring communication event on a construction project. What you say — or fail to say — can become part of the project record and may surface in claims years later.

---

### 2.2 Pre-Bid Meeting

**Purpose:** Prospective bidders tour the site, ask questions, and receive clarifications from the owner and design team.

**Key rules:**
- May be **mandatory** — non-attendance disqualifies the bidder
- Q&A is documented and distributed as **addenda** to all bidders
- **Verbal statements do not modify the contract** unless confirmed in a written addendum

---

### 2.3 Pre-Construction Meeting

**Purpose:** Held after contract award, before work begins. Establishes the ground rules.

**Typical topics:**
- Communication protocols (who contacts whom, response timeframes)
- Submittal schedule and RFI procedures
- Schedule of values and payment application process
- Safety plan and site logistics
- Document management system and meeting schedule

---

### 2.4 Safety Meeting / Toolbox Talk

**Purpose:** Brief, focused safety briefings on hazards specific to current work.

**Key features:**
- **10–15 minutes** at the start of the shift
- Covers specific hazards for the day's planned work
- **Attendance documented** (sign-in sheet) — OSHA may request these records
- Topics: fall protection, trenching/shoring, hot work permits, crane operations, confined space

---

### 2.5 Progress / Look-Ahead Meeting

**Purpose:** Internal coordination focused on the **three-week look-ahead**. The GC team and sub foremen identify scheduling conflicts before they become delays.

**Typical agenda:**
- Work completed in the past week
- Three-week look-ahead — activities, crews, material deliveries
- Scheduling conflicts — trade stacking, access, sequencing
- Material and equipment status — lead times, delivery confirmations
- Manpower — planned vs. actual crew sizes

> **Takeaway:** If the OAC meeting is the boardroom, the look-ahead meeting is the **engine room**. Problems identified here get resolved quietly. Problems missed here show up as delays and disputes at the OAC level.

## Part III: Running an Effective Meeting

Meeting effectiveness depends on what happens **before** even more than what happens during.

---

### 3.1 Before the Meeting

**Circulate an agenda at least 24 hours in advance.**
- An agenda signals that this meeting has a purpose
- The standard OAC agenda (Section 2.1) serves as a standing template, updated weekly

**Attach supporting documents.**
- Updated schedule, RFI log, change order log, submittal register
- If participants must review material to contribute — give it to them in advance, not as a surprise

**Define roles:**

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| **Chair** | Runs the agenda, manages time, keeps discussion on track |
| **Note-taker** | Captures decisions, action items, attendance |

Rotate the note-taker role among junior staff — excellent training in listening and synthesis.

**Set a hard time limit:**
- 60 minutes for an OAC meeting
- 30 minutes for internal progress meetings
- Meetings without time constraints expand to fill available time

---

### 3.2 During the Meeting

**Start on time, regardless of who is absent.**
Waiting for latecomers punishes the people who showed up on time.

**Follow the agenda.**
When discussion drifts, use a **parking lot** — a visible list of items to address separately.

**Assign every item an owner and a due date.**
This is the **single most important discipline** in meeting management. Without it, discussions produce conversation but not action.

**Summarize each decision out loud before moving on.**
- Confirms everyone agrees on what was decided
- Gives the note-taker an explicit statement to record

---

### Managing Multi-Party Dynamics

Construction meetings often involve parties with **competing interests**: the owner wants to minimize cost, the contractor wants to protect margin, the architect wants design integrity.

**Useful phrases:**
- *"Let's focus on the facts first before we discuss responsibility."*
- *"I understand the concern. Can we agree on the timeline and address the cost implications separately?"*
- *"Let's document this disagreement accurately so both positions are on the record."*

> **Takeaway:** The construction industry has a well-documented pattern of adversarial relations among project parties. The OAC meeting is where you can either reinforce or break that pattern. The chair sets the tone.

*Source: Dainty, A., Moore, D. & Murray, M. (2006). Communication in Construction: Theory and Practice. Taylor & Francis.*

---

### 3.3 After the Meeting

**Distribute minutes within 24 hours.**
Delay erodes accuracy — the longer you wait, the less reliable the record.

**Highlight action items.** Every action item must include:
- **What** is to be done
- **Who** is responsible
- **By when** (specific date)

**Include the acceptance clause:**
> *"If no objections are received within [5–7] business days, these minutes will be considered accepted as the official record."*

This is a **contractual mechanism**, not a courtesy. (See Part V.)

**Track open items across meetings.**
Carry unresolved items forward to the next meeting's agenda. An action item log — a running list with status tracking — prevents items from falling through the cracks.

## Part IV: Speaking Up in Meetings

Graduate students and junior engineers often sit silently through meetings dominated by senior people.

**This is understandable. It is also a mistake.**

If you see a problem and say nothing, you own part of that problem.

Here are three concrete techniques.

---

### 4.1 The Structured Comment

**Format: Context → Point → Implication**

**Good:**
> "Looking at the three-week look-ahead **[context]**, the electrical rough-in on Level 2 conflicts with the drywall start date **[point]**. If we don't resolve this before next week, drywall will slip by at least a week, which puts the Level 2 finishes on the critical path **[implication]**."

**Bad:**
> "I think there might be a problem with Level 2."

The first version prompts a decision. The second prompts a shrug.

---

### 4.2 The Clarifying Question

When you do not understand something, asking is a **professional obligation** — not a weakness. Confusion that goes unvoiced becomes an error that goes uncorrected.

**Example:**
> "Can you clarify which spec section governs the curtain wall anchor embedment depth? I want to make sure the submittal we are reviewing references the correct revision, because the structural drawings were updated in Addendum 3."

A good clarifying question is **specific**, **references a document**, and **explains why you are asking**.

---

### 4.3 The Flagging Statement

When you see a risk others may have missed — flag it explicitly. Do not wait for someone else.

**Example:**
> "I want to flag that the concrete pour is scheduled for Friday, but the weather forecast shows rain Friday afternoon. If we proceed without a contingency plan — protective covers, rescheduling, or an accelerated pour — we risk a cold joint or surface defects."

---

### 4.4 What to Avoid

| Don't | Why |
|-------|-----|
| *"Sorry, this might be a dumb question..."* | Signals low confidence, invites dismissal |
| *"I think there might be an issue with something..."* | If you cannot name the issue, you are not ready to speak |
| Long narratives with no clear point | Every minute costs money. Earn your airtime. |

> **Takeaway:** The most respected people in construction meetings are not the loudest. They speak rarely but always with a point, always with facts, and always with a clear implication. Aspire to be that person.

## Part V: Meeting Minutes — The Official Record

Meeting minutes in construction are not a transcript. They are not a summary.

They are **the official project record** — a legal document that captures what was decided, by whom, and when.

---

### 5.1 Legal Significance

- Courts treat contemporaneous business records — including meeting minutes — as **prima facie evidence** of what occurred
- In construction litigation, meeting minutes are among the **most frequently cited documents** — they establish a timeline of decisions, notifications, and agreements
- Inaccurate minutes may **undermine the credibility** of other documentation, even if the issue is documented elsewhere
- Well-written minutes can **prevent claims from escalating to litigation** by removing ambiguity about what was agreed upon

*Sources: [Shutts & Bowen LLP: Effective Documentation Techniques for Construction Project Participants](https://www.shutts.com/business-and-legal-insights/effective-documentation-techniques-for-construction-project-participants); [ACS Lawyers: Keeping Effective Construction Meeting Minutes](https://acslawyers.com/claims/keeping-effective-construction-meeting-minutes/); [Long International: Preserving Your Claim through Documentation](https://www.long-intl.com/articles/preserving-your-claim/)*

---

### 5.2 The Silence-Is-Acceptance Clause

The sentence at the bottom of every set of construction meeting minutes:

> *"If no objections are received within 7 days, these minutes will be considered accepted as the official record."*

**This is one of the most powerful lines in CEM communication.**

---

### Why It Works

- Under general contract law, silence does **not** constitute acceptance
- **However:** when parties have an established course of dealing (recurring meetings with consistent documentation), and the protocols explicitly state that silence = acceptance, **courts have recognized this mechanism**
- If you receive minutes you disagree with and say nothing → the disputed content may be treated as the **accepted record**

> **Takeaway:** The most common cause of meeting minutes disputes is not falsification — it is **neglect**. People skim the minutes (or ignore them), then discover months later during a claim that the record does not match their memory. By then, it is too late.

**Always read the minutes. Always.**

---

### 5.3 Required Elements

Every set of construction meeting minutes must include:

| # | Element | Notes |
|---|---------|-------|
| 1 | **Header** | Project name, meeting number, date, time, location |
| 2 | **Attendance** | Present and absent (with notation if absence was notified) |
| 3 | **Approval of previous minutes** | Accepted, or corrections noted |
| 4 | **Discussion items** | Organized by agenda topic — substance, not verbatim |
| 5 | **Decisions made** | What was agreed upon, stated clearly |
| 6 | **Action items** | Who + what + by when (name + date, every time) |
| 7 | **Open items** | Unresolved issues carried forward, with origin meeting reference |
| 8 | **Next meeting** | Date, time, location |
| 9 | **Prepared by / Distribution list** | |
| 10 | **Acceptance clause** | Silence-is-acceptance with a specific deadline |

---

### 5.4 Example Template

```
================================================================
MEETING MINUTES
Project: Bogazici Research Center — Phase 2
Meeting: OAC Meeting #14
Date: March 19, 2026, 10:00–11:15
Location: Site Trailer, Conference Room A
================================================================

ATTENDEES:
  S. Ozkan (Owner's Rep), B. Celik (Project Architect),
  A. Yilmaz (GC–PM), M. Demir (GC–Superintendent),
  K. Aydin (Electrical Sub)

ABSENT:
  R. Kara (Structural Engineer) — notified in advance

PREVIOUS MINUTES: OAC #13 minutes accepted without objection.

---------------------------------------------------------------
1. SCHEDULE UPDATE
---------------------------------------------------------------
- Project is 2 days ahead of baseline schedule.
- Level 3 slab pour completed March 15.
- Decision: Move Level 4 formwork start to March 22 (2 days
  earlier than planned).

---------------------------------------------------------------
2. OPEN RFIs
---------------------------------------------------------------
- RFI-047 (rebar spacing at L3 transfer beam): CLOSED.
  Option B approved per structural engineer's response 3/12.
- RFI-052 (curtain wall anchor detail): OPEN.
  >> Action: B. Celik to respond to RFI-052 by March 24.

---------------------------------------------------------------
3. CHANGE ORDERS
---------------------------------------------------------------
- CO-008 (fire suppression modification, parking level):
  Owner reviewing. Estimated cost: $42,000.
  >> Action: S. Ozkan to approve or reject CO-008 by March 26.

---------------------------------------------------------------
4. SAFETY
---------------------------------------------------------------
- No recordable incidents since last meeting.
- Upcoming: crane mobilization for L4 steel erection week of
  March 24. Safety plan to be reviewed at toolbox talk 3/24 AM.

---------------------------------------------------------------
5. NEW BUSINESS
---------------------------------------------------------------
- K. Aydin raised concern about electrical panel lead time
  (12 weeks). Current delivery date: June 15.
  >> Action: K. Aydin to confirm delivery date with supplier
     and report back at OAC #15.

---------------------------------------------------------------
OPEN ITEMS (carried forward):
  - RFI-052: curtain wall anchor (due 3/24)
  - CO-008: fire suppression (due 3/26)
  - Elevator pit waterproofing detail (from OAC #12, pending
    architect response)

NEXT MEETING: March 26, 2026, 10:00 AM, Site Trailer

Prepared by: A. Yilmaz
Distribution: All attendees + R. Kara

If no written objections are received by March 26, 2026,
these minutes will be accepted as the official project record.
================================================================
```

---

### What Makes This Template Work

> Notice that every action item has a **name** and a **date**. "The architect will look into it" is **not** an action item. "B. Celik to respond to RFI-052 by March 24" **is**. If you cannot assign a name and a date, the item is still a discussion topic — not an action item.

## In-Class Activity: Mock OAC Meeting (50 min)

---

### Setup (5 min)

Teams of 5–6 students simulate **OAC Meeting #15** for the Bogazici Research Center project.

**Roles** (assigned randomly):
- Owner's Representative
- Project Architect
- GC Project Manager (chairs the meeting)
- GC Superintendent
- Electrical Subcontractor
- Structural Engineer (may be absent — at instructor's discretion)

See `scenarios/oac_meeting_scenario.md` for the full project status and each role's priorities.

---

### Conduct (30 min)

- The GC PM runs the meeting using the standard OAC agenda from Section 2.1
- Each participant must make at least **two substantive contributions** using the techniques from Part IV
- At least one **built-in conflict** must be surfaced and discussed
- One student (not playing a role) serves as the **note-taker**, capturing minutes in real time

---

### Debrief (15 min)

The class observes and critiques:

- Who communicated clearly? Who used **Context → Point → Implication**?
- Who got sidetracked or spoke without a clear point?
- Did the meeting produce **clear, assignable action items**?
- Did the chair manage time and multi-party dynamics effectively?
- Would the resulting minutes hold up as a **contractual record**?

## Homework: Meeting Minutes

**Due:** Before Week 8

**Assignment:** Write formal meeting minutes for the mock OAC meeting you participated in (or observed).

**Requirements:**
1. Follow the template format from Section 5.4
2. Include all required elements from Section 5.3
3. List all attendees and absentees
4. Capture at least 3 decisions or outcomes
5. Include at least 4 action items — each with a responsible party and a specific due date
6. Include the silence-is-acceptance clause with a stated deadline
7. Maximum 2 pages

**Grading:** Letter grade (A/B/C/F) based on:
- **Completeness** — Are all required elements present?
- **Clarity** — Could someone who was not at the meeting understand what happened?
- **Contractual usefulness** — Would this document hold up as a reliable project record?
